## Overview

Several AMC 8 problems each year present data in a visual format and ask you to
extract, compare, or compute with it. This module trains careful reading of bar
graphs, histograms, pie charts, and line graphs.

## Key Ideas

- Read axis labels and units before looking at the data.
- Histograms show frequency distributions; the height of each bar tells you how
  many data points fall in that interval.
- Pie charts represent parts of a whole; each slice is a percentage of the total.
- Watch for broken axes or non-uniform scales designed to mislead.

## Core Skills

### Reading Bar Graphs

Identify the category, read the height against the scale, and compare values. If the top falls between two grid lines, estimate the value based on the scale.

### Interpreting Pie Charts

Each sector's central angle equals $\frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} \times 360°$.
Convert between angle, fraction, and percent. Sectors are also proportional to the data they represent, so a bigger slice means a bigger percentage.

### Multi-Step Data Questions

Some problems require combining data from different parts of the same chart or
from two different charts. Often you will find the problems asking for the total of two parts.

## Worked Example

A pie chart shows that 25% of students prefer soccer. If there are 120 students
total, how many prefer soccer?

$25\%$ of $120 = 0.25 \times 120 = 30$ students.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Pie Chart

In a pie chart showing the number of pets owned by 50 families, the sector for "2 pets" had a degree measure of $72^{\circ}$. What percentage of the families own exactly 2 pets?

The total degree measure of the pie chart is $360^{\circ}$. Therefore, the percentage is just $\frac{72}{360}=\frac{1}{5}=\frac{20}{100}=20%$.

### Example 2: Bar Graphs

A bar graph displays the scores of a class on a 10-point quiz. There are bars for scores of 7, 8, 9, and 10 with heights of 4, 6, 8, and 2, respectively. What is the average  score of the students represented in this histogram?

To find the average, we need to sum all of the scores and divide by the total number of scores. The total number of scores is the sum of the heights of the bars which is $4+6+8+2=20$. The sum of all the scores can be calculated by multiplying each score by its frequency. We get $7 \cdot 4 + 8 \cdot 6 + 9 \cdot 8 + 2 \cdot 10 = 168$. Finally, we can divide the total sum of the scores by the total number of students, giving us $\frac{168}{20}=8.4$. Therefore, the average score of all of the students is 8.4.
